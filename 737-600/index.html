Boeing 737-600

The Rare "Baby Boeing" of the Next Generation Family

🚀 Overview

The Boeing 737-600 is the smallest, shortest, and rarest member of the Boeing 737 Next Generation (NG) airliner family. Developed in the mid-1990s as the direct successor to the classic 737-500, it was engineered to serve short-to-medium-haul routes with lower passenger demand. Despite receiving all the advanced technological enhancements of the NG series—including modern glass cockpits, a redesigned wing, and new turbofan engines—it became the slowest-selling airliner in the entire Next Generation lineup, with only 69 units ever produced.

📊 Technical Specifications

Designed for efficiency on thin routes, the 737-600 combined a compact fuselage with high thrust-to-weight performance.

📉 Why Did the 737-600 Fail?

The primary reason for the 737-600's commercial failure is found in basic airline economics. While Boeing successfully shrunk the fuselage, the aircraft still shared the same wings, landing gear, tail structure, and engines as the larger 737-700. This meant that the 737-600 was structurally heavy relative to its seating capacity. It carried substantial "dead weight" without the ability to offset it with passenger ticket sales.

The Dilemma: An airline operating the 737-700 could carry up to 20 more passengers on almost the exact same fuel budget, with the same flight crew requirements and maintenance costs. As a result, carriers overwhelmingly selected the larger siblings, driving Boeing to eventually remove the 737-600 from its catalog in 2012.

✈️ Operators: A Rare Sight in the Skies

Today, the 737-600 is highly sought after by aviation enthusiasts due to its rarity. Most commercial airlines, such as launch customer Scandinavian Airlines (SAS) and WestJet, retired their fleets in the late 2010s. However, a few unique fleets still maintain operations:

🇩🇿 Air Algérie Active

The state-owned flag carrier of Algeria operates a small fleet of 737-600s. These aircraft are utilized on regional and domestic routes where passenger volumes are lower but runway capabilities demand jet transport.

🇺🇸 "Janet" Airlines Classified

The United States Air Force operates several unmarked 737-600s out of Las Vegas McCarran International Airport. Known colloquially as "Janet", these planes ferry government employees and contractors to highly restricted sites, including Area 51 and the Tonopah Test Range.

🇨🇦 KF Aerospace / Cargo Specialist

Based in Kelowna, Canada, KF Cargo has utilized 737-600 variants for charter flights and specialist operations, capitalizing on their high structural strength and short-runway capabilities.
